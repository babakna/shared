How to use this planner v2.0
People & birth years
Names
Enter names for the Husband, Wife, and Child. All labels update throughout the app. Names are saved with scenarios.
Birth years
Used to calculate exact SS milestone dates. More accurate than ages. FRA is automatically determined from the husband's birth year (67 for those born 1960 or later).
Life expectancy
Age each person is assumed to live to. The model runs until the last surviving person passes. The winner scenario often changes significantly based on the wife's longevity.
Social Security — Husband
FRA benefit (PIA)
Primary Insurance Amount — what SS pays at Full Retirement Age. Find your actual number at ssa.gov/myaccount. All other SS amounts are calculated from this.
File at 62
Permanently reduces benefit to 70% of FRA (for FRA=67). Triggers the child benefit immediately (up to 5 years for a 12-year-old). Reduces wife's survivor benefit permanently.
File at 65
Moderate reduction — roughly 86.7% of FRA (for FRA=67). Balances earlier income against a smaller permanent reduction. Child may still get a few years of benefit.
File at 67 (FRA)
Full benefit, no reduction or bonus. Child benefit window closes — child turns 18 around this time. Clean baseline with no penalties.
File at 70
Maximum benefit at 124% of FRA. Best outcome for wife's survivor benefit over a long horizon. For a non-DAC minor child the benefit window is largely closed (child likely >18 by then); for a DAC-qualified child the 50%-PIA benefit begins when the parent files and continues for life.
Pass without filing
Family is still protected. Wife gets survivor based on FRA (or higher if husband passed after FRA with delayed credits accumulated). Husband collects $0 while alive.
Dies before file age
If you set husband's death age earlier than a scenario's filing age (e.g., death at 64 with File at 67), the model treats that scenario as a no-filing case: husband collected $0, no Widow's Limit applies, and the wife receives a survivor benefit based on what husband would have qualified for at the time of death (full PIA if before FRA; PIA + delayed credits if after FRA, capped at age 70).
Social Security — Wife (spousal & survivor)
Spousal benefit
50% of husband's FRA benefit (PIA). Available only after husband files AND wife is at least 62. The spousal amount is always based on husband's PIA — his filing age (62, 65, 67, 70) does NOT change the spousal amount. v10 applies the early-claim reduction (POMS RS 00615.201): 25/36 of 1% per month for the first 36 months early, 5/12 of 1% per month beyond. At age 62 with FRA 67, spousal = 32.5% of PIA (not 50%).
Spousal eligibility
Requires: (1) husband has filed for SS, (2) wife is at least 62, (3) married for at least 1 year. If these conditions are not met, wife receives $0 spousal (she may still collect her own SS if she has credits).
Survivor benefit
Up to 100% of whatever husband was collecting when he passes. This IS directly affected by husband's filing age — if he filed at 62, the survivor base is lower than if he filed at 70. Widow's Limit (RIB-LIM): if husband filed early, survivor = MAX(his reduced benefit, 82.5% of PIA).
Survivor timing
Claimable as early as age 60 (or 50 if disabled). Claiming before FRA (67) permanently reduces the survivor amount: 71.5% at age 60, scaling linearly to 100% at age 67. This model applies that reduction based on when the wife first becomes eligible to claim.
Spousal vs survivor
While husband is alive: wife gets spousal (50% PIA). After husband passes: wife switches to survivor (up to 100% of husband's benefit). SS always pays the higher of spousal, survivor, or wife's own benefit — never combined.
Own SS credits
If wife has her own work record, SS pays the higher of her own benefit or spousal/survivor — never both combined. She collects her own benefit from age 62 independently if she has credits, even if husband hasn't filed yet.
Social Security — Child (DAC)
Child benefit
50% of husband's FRA (PIA) while he is alive and has filed. For non-disabled minors: ends at age 18 (SSA allows 19 if still in high school — this model uses a hard 18 cutoff for simplicity). v10: for a DAC-qualified child, the 50% benefit continues for life while the parent is alive and filed (POMS DI 10115.001), then switches to the 75% DAC survivor amount on the parent's death.
DAC survivor
Disabled Adult Child benefit — 75% of husband's PIA, for child's entire lifetime after husband passes. Requires disability onset documented before age 22. The DAC amount is always based on the husband's PIA regardless of when he filed. Grows with COLA annually. While the parent is alive and filed, the DAC instead receives 50% of PIA (the child-benefit amount), with no age-18 cutoff.
DAC eligibility
Child must be unmarried, have a disability that began before age 22, and meet SSA's definition of disability. Apply through SSA — documentation of early-onset disability is critical. Call SSA at 1-800-772-1213 (TTY 1-800-325-0778) or visit ssa.gov/benefits/disability.
Family Maximum
v10 applies the FMB cap. SSA caps total family benefits at ~150–187% of PIA via a four-bracket formula on monthly PIA (POMS RS 00615.768). Auxiliaries (spousal + child + DAC while parent alive) are prorated when their sum exceeds FMB − worker's PIA. Survivor benefits after the worker passes are NOT subject to FMB (they come from a different fund). Verify with SSA for exact amounts.
Living expenses
Child's base annual cost when both parents are gone — housing, food, medical, caregivers beyond Medicaid. In today's dollars.
SNT admin costs
Annual cost to administer the Special Needs Trust — corporate trustee fees (0.5–1% of assets), CPA, rep payee, legal reviews. Typically $5K–$20K/yr. Added to living expenses when both parents are gone.
Representative Payee
SSA requires a Representative Payee to receive and manage SS/DAC payments on behalf of a beneficiary who cannot manage their own funds. The payee must keep records of how benefits are spent and file annual accounting (Form SSA-6230). A parent, family member, or corporate trustee can serve as payee. After both parents pass, the SNT trustee typically becomes rep payee. See: ssa.gov/payee | SSA Pub 10076 (Guide for Rep Payees) | SSA Pub 10098 (Benefits for Children with Disabilities) | POMS GN 00502 (Rep Payee policy).
Taxes
Family tax rate
Effective tax rate on portfolio withdrawals and SS income while parents are alive. Default 22%. Applied to the gap between spending needs and SS income received.
SNT tax rate
Trust tax rate after both parents pass. SNT trusts reach the 37% bracket very quickly (at ~$15K of income in 2024). Default 37%. Applied to portfolio draws needed beyond child's SS and DAC income.
Portfolio & spending
Family spending
Total annual household spending while at least one parent is alive. All amounts in today's dollars — the model inflates them forward automatically.
Today's $ vs Nominal
Today's dollars adjusts all figures back to today's purchasing power — easier to understand. Nominal shows actual dollar amounts as they grow with inflation over time.
Annual return
Expected average portfolio return. 7–8% is a common long-run assumption. Reduce to 5–6% for a conservative stress test. The model assumes constant returns — real outcomes will vary.
Scenarios
Save scenario
Save up to 3 named scenarios. Each stores all input values. Click a saved slot to load, rename, or delete it. When 2+ are saved, a comparison table appears automatically.
Winner
The filing scenario leaving the largest portfolio at end of plan — accounting for SS income, taxes, portfolio growth, and spending across the full lifetime of all three family members.
v10 — Sensitivity, Monte Carlo & survivor-timing panels
Sensitivity Grid A
Heatmap: rows = wife's death age (80–100), cols = portfolio return rate (4–10%). Each cell shows the best filing age for that combination. Gray cells = all filing ages run out of money. Helps identify whether your winner is robust to longevity and market assumptions.
Sensitivity Grid B
Heatmap: rows = husband's death age (73–93), cols = wife's death age (80–100). The single most decision-critical pairing — file-at-70 typically wins when the husband lives long; file-at-62 wins when he dies early. Use this to see how the winning strategy depends on lifespan assumptions you can't actually know.
Monte Carlo
Runs 500 random trials per filing age with year-by-year returns drawn from a normal distribution around your assumed return (σ=12%, typical for a balanced portfolio). Addresses sequence-of-returns risk that the constant-return model misses. Reports: success rate (% of trials where portfolio survives), 10th/median/90th percentile final values. The "robust winner" is the filing age with the highest survival rate — often different from the constant-return winner. Wide spreads = high sensitivity to return luck.
Survivor timing
When the wife has her own SS benefit, SSA allows her to claim her own benefit and survivor benefit at different ages — switching from one to the other later. This panel compares five common strategies (e.g., "survivor at 60, switch to own at 70" vs "own at 62, switch to survivor at FRA") and shows lifetime total for each. Only appears when wife's own SS > $0. POMS RS 00615.482.
Fractional FRA
For birth years 1938–1942 and 1955–1959, FRA is partial-month (e.g., 1957 = 66 + 6 mo). v10 uses the exact fractional FRA in all calculations and displays it as "66 + 6 mo" wherever the FRA appears. Birth years 1960+ have FRA = 67 (whole).
Important: This is a planning model, not financial advice. Assumes constant returns (deterministic scenarios) or random returns (Monte Carlo only), average COLA, and exact life expectancies. Real outcomes vary. Consult a fee-only financial advisor and special needs attorney for your specific situation.
