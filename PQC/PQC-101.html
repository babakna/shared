Why Quantum Computing Changes the Threat Model

The risk is not "encryption dies overnight." It is a specific, uneven threat with a timing twist that makes waiting dangerous. This module makes the logic concrete — and lets you compute the urgency yourself.

  • Explain why symmetric encryption and hashes are affected differently.
  • Prioritize systems by data lifetime, exposure, and migration time.

Nobody knows the real "Q-day." The point of the tool is the relationship: if data lifetime exceeds time-to-quantum, captured traffic is exposed while it still matters — and migration time eats into your runway.

1

What a "cryptographically relevant quantum computer" means

Not any quantum computer — a big, reliable, error-corrected one able to break deployed public-key crypto in practical time.

The exact arrival date (often called Q-day) is genuinely uncertain, and credible estimates span a wide range. Security planning does not require a precise date. It requires reasoning under uncertainty: if the consequence is severe and the fix takes years, waiting for certainty is not responsible.

Two words in that definition carry all the weight: error-corrected and practical time. A machine that can run Shor once, slowly, on a toy number is a scientific milestone but not a threat. A "cryptographically relevant" machine can run Shor against a real 2048-bit key and finish before the answer stops mattering. The distance between those two is measured in orders of magnitude of reliable, error-corrected qubits — which is exactly why credible arrival estimates range from roughly a decade to "much longer," and why no honest expert gives you a single confident date.

Why the uncertainty is not an excuse to wait

Imagine a fire that might reach your building in 10 to 30 years, and evacuating everything takes 8. You would not wait for the smoke. You would start moving the irreplaceable items now. Cryptographic migration works the same way: the fix is slow, so the trigger to act is the combination of consequence and lead time, not a confirmed date.

It helps to separate three things people constantly blur together: a quantum computer exists, a quantum computer is cryptographically relevant, and a quantum computer is in the hands of an adversary targeting you. Today we have the first (small, noisy machines). The second is the milestone this module cares about. The third is a further step that may lag the second by years for most organizations — but for the highest-value targets, you should assume a capable nation-state is first in line. Your risk tier decides which of these thresholds you plan against.

Reframe the question

Don't ask "Is Q-day tomorrow?" Ask "Which of my data and trust systems would still matter by the time my migration is finished?" That question you can answer today.

2

Shor's algorithm versus Grover's algorithm

One algorithm is a demolition charge for public-key crypto; the other is a slow leak you can patch with bigger keys.

Shor → public-key collapses

Breaks RSA, finite-field Diffie-Hellman, ECDH, ECDSA, and EdDSA by efficiently solving factoring and discrete logs. These do not survive a CRQC. They must be replaced or hybridized.

Grover → margins shrink

Gives a square-root speedup for brute-force search. Symmetric keys and hashes lose roughly half their effective bits. AES-256 and SHA-384/512-class outputs keep strong margins.

Keep the contrast sharp, because it drives your whole strategy: Shor is a structural attack that dismantles specific public-key math, so those algorithms are finished and must be replaced; Grover is a brute-force speedup that just halves your effective symmetric strength, so you compensate simply by doubling key sizes (AES-128 → AES-256). One demands new algorithms; the other demands bigger numbers you already have. That is why "quantum breaks encryption" is a misleading headline — it breaks one family and mildly dents the other.

Common pitfall

Telling stakeholders "quantum breaks all encryption equally." It does not, and that message creates panic and bad priorities. The precise message: replace classical public-key; review symmetric/hash sizes.

3

Harvest now, decrypt later

The attacker does not need a quantum computer today to hurt you with one tomorrow.

Harvest now, decrypt later means an adversary records your encrypted traffic or copies encrypted files now and simply waits. When a CRQC arrives, they decrypt the archive. The theft happened today; the damage lands in the future — but only if the data is still sensitive then.

Concrete example

Encrypted traffic carrying merger documents or 25-year medical records is captured today. If the key exchange is broken in 12 years and those documents still matter, the breach is real — even though nothing looked wrong at capture time.

Not all data is worth harvesting. A well-resourced adversary chooses targets by value × lifetime: they store what will still be damaging years from now. That is why some data types are classic harvest targets and others are not. State secrets, intelligence, and diplomatic cables (secret for decades); genomic and medical records (secret for a lifetime); trade secrets, source code, and unreleased designs; long-term financial and legal records; and the master keys that protect all of the above. By contrast, a one-day-old news feed, a public price list, or an already-published document has essentially zero harvest value.

The uncomfortable part

You cannot detect harvesting. Copying encrypted traffic leaves no trace on your systems, and the attacker never has to touch your servers. There is no alert, no log, no breach notification — which is exactly why the defense has to be proactive migration rather than detection.

Common pitfall

Ranking risk only by "is it internet-facing?" Long-lived internal secrets can outrank a public marketing site.

4

Practical risk triage

A first-pass score, not a final cryptographic review, tells you where to start.

FactorQuestionRaises priority when...
Data lifetimeHow long must it stay secret / trusted?Years to decades.
ExposureCan an attacker capture it?Crosses untrusted networks or third parties.
Public-key dependenceDoes it rely on RSA/ECC for key exchange or signatures?Yes, and hard to change.
Business impactWhat if it is exposed or forged?Regulated, safety-critical, or reputational.
Migration difficultyHow long to fix?Vendor-controlled, embedded, or long-lived.

High-priority areas usually include external TLS, VPN, SSH, identity systems, code and firmware signing, certificate authorities, HSM/KMS dependencies, long-lived encrypted archives, and slow-to-update vendor products. Low-priority does not mean "ignore" — it means inventory, monitor, and migrate on the normal refresh cycle.

Takeaway

Prioritization prevents both panic and neglect. Some systems need urgent architecture review; others just need to be tracked.

5

Mosca's inequality: the one formula that decides urgency

Three numbers tell you whether you are already behind — no crystal ball required.

Cryptographer Michele Mosca framed the whole timing problem as a simple comparison. Let X be how long your data must stay secure, Y be how long your migration will take, and Z be the time until a cryptographically relevant quantum computer arrives. The uncomfortable rule is: if X + Y > Z, you have a problem right now, because data protected today will still matter when it becomes decryptable.

This is exactly what the calculator at the top of the module computes. Notice which levers you actually control: you cannot move Q-day (Z), but you can shrink your migration time (Y) by starting early and building crypto-agility, and you can sometimes shorten data lifetime (X) by re-encrypting or rotating. Every month you delay adds directly to the exposure gap.

Common pitfall

Assuming a comfortable Z ("quantum is 20 years away, relax"). Even a generous Z can be smaller than X + Y for long-lived data in a large organization — and Z estimates have only ever gotten shorter, never longer.

6

Communicating the risk without causing panic

Half of this job is saying the right thing to executives, auditors, and engineers.

The quantum threat is unusually easy to communicate badly. Say too little and nobody funds the multi-year program; say too much and you trigger a "rip everything out now" panic that wastes money on low-risk systems. The fix is a precise, proportionate message tuned to the audience.

Say thisNot this
"Public-key crypto (key exchange and signatures) must be replaced over the next several years.""Quantum computers will break all our encryption overnight."
"Symmetric encryption and hashes mostly just need larger sizes.""Everything cryptographic is equally doomed."
"Long-lived, exposed data is at risk today via harvest-now-decrypt-later.""There's no risk until Q-day arrives."
"We prioritize by data lifetime, exposure, and migration difficulty.""We'll migrate everything at once, alphabetically."
"This is a multi-year, agility-building program — start now, move steadily.""One project, done next quarter."
Executive one-liner

"A future quantum computer will break the public-key cryptography that secures our identities, connections, and signatures. The fix takes years and some of our data is already being harvested for later decryption, so we are starting a prioritized, multi-year migration now." That single sentence funds the program without causing panic.

Takeaway

Accuracy is the antidote to both complacency and hysteria. Name the specific threat, the specific fix, and the specific priority order — every time.

Hands-on labs

Decide first, then reveal.

Lab 1 · VPN vs marketing site

You can only migrate one system this quarter: the corporate VPN carrying internal traffic, or the public marketing website.

Which goes first, and why?

Recommended answer The VPN. It carries long-lived, sensitive internal data over untrusted networks and depends on public-key key exchange — a textbook harvest-now-decrypt-later target. The marketing site's content is public and short-lived.

Lab 2 · Old signed firmware

Field devices accept firmware signed with a classical signature and will run for 10+ years.

Why can signatures be urgent even with no confidential data involved?

Recommended answer If the signature algorithm is broken during the device's life, an attacker could forge updates and take over the fleet. Long-lived trust roots and verifiers must move early even when confidentiality is irrelevant.

Lab 3 · Short-lived news page

A public news page serves content that is irrelevant within a day.

First migration wave, or track it?

Recommended answer Track it. Nothing is secret and nothing is long-lived, so harvest-now-decrypt-later does not apply. Migrate it on the normal refresh cycle behind higher-risk systems.

Lab 4 · Apply Mosca's inequality

A system's data must stay secret for 15 years (X), migration will take 6 years (Y), and your planning estimate for Q-day is 10 years (Z).

Do you have a problem today?

Recommended answer Yes. X + Y = 21, which is greater than Z = 10, so data encrypted today would be exposed for about 11 years after Q-day while still sensitive. You are already behind and should start immediately — and note that even doubling your Q-day estimate to 20 still leaves X + Y > Z.

Lab 5 · Brief the board

An executive reads a headline and asks: "Should we panic and replace all our encryption this quarter?"

What is the accurate, calming, funded answer?

Recommended answer "No panic, but yes action. The threat is specific: public-key key exchange and signatures must be replaced over several years; symmetric encryption mostly needs larger keys. Some long-lived data is already at risk from harvest-now-decrypt-later, so we are starting a prioritized, multi-year migration now — not a one-quarter rip-and-replace."
