Session 0: What Cryptography Is For

Cryptography is the engineering discipline of protecting information and proving trust in hostile environments. It is not just "hiding messages." Modern cryptography lets systems communicate securely even when attackers can watch the network, modify traffic, impersonate users, or steal old data.

The four jobs crypto usually performs
ConfidentialityOnly intended parties can read the data.
IntegrityChanges to data can be detected.
AuthenticationA system can prove who or what it is talking to.
Non-repudiationA signature can prove an action came from a specific key holder.
A simple mental model

Encryption is the locked box. A key is what opens or creates the box. A signature is a tamper-evident seal. A certificate is an identity card signed by someone trusted.

Why it matters

Without cryptography, online banking, software updates, cellular authentication, secure websites, private messaging, cloud backup, and digital identity would not be trustworthy at internet scale.

A Short History: How We Got Here

Cryptography moved from manual secrecy to machine-era ciphers, then to mathematically grounded public-key systems. PQC is the next transition because the assumptions behind RSA and elliptic-curve systems are exposed to quantum algorithms.

Historical progression
Classical ciphersSubstitution and transposition; useful for teaching, weak against analysis.
Mechanical cryptoRotor machines and operational security showed crypto is also a system problem.
Symmetric eraDES, AES, and modern authenticated encryption protect bulk data efficiently.
Public-key eraRSA, Diffie-Hellman, and ECC make internet-scale key exchange and signatures practical.
PKI/web eraCertificates, TLS, code signing, and device identity connect trust to networks.
PQC transitionQuantum-resistant public-key algorithms replace the vulnerable public-key layer.

Core Building Blocks

Most real systems combine several primitives. PQC mostly changes the public-key pieces: key establishment and signatures. Symmetric encryption and hashes remain central, usually with adequate key sizes and parameters.

Primitive map
S
Symmetric encryption

One secret key encrypts and decrypts data. Example use: file or session encryption.

K
Key exchange / KEM

Two parties establish a shared secret over a public network.

D
Digital signatures

A private key signs; a public key verifies authenticity and integrity.

H
Hashes

One-way fingerprints of data. Used in integrity, signatures, logs, and commitments.

C
Certificates

Bind a public key to an identity through a chain of trust.

Module 1: Why Quantum Changes the Plan

Post-quantum cryptography means classical algorithms designed to remain secure against both classical and quantum attackers. It does not run on a quantum computer. It replaces the public-key math that a sufficiently capable quantum computer could break.

What changes under the quantum threat
RSA, DH, ECC

Threatened by Shor's algorithm. These are the main public-key systems that need replacement.

AES, hashes, MACs

Not broken the same way. Use conservative key sizes and modern constructions.

Long-life data

Harvest-now-decrypt-later makes migration urgent before a large quantum computer exists.

Practical rule: prioritize data whose confidentiality lifetime is longer than your migration timeline. Medical, legal, government, telecom, finance, identity, firmware signing, and long-lived IP usually move first.

PQC Standards and Algorithm Families

NIST finalized the first three Federal Information Processing Standards in 2024. These are the main algorithms to learn and plan around now. NIST also continues work on backup and alternative algorithms.

FIPS 203

ML-KEM

Primary key-establishment mechanism. Derived from CRYSTALS-Kyber and based on module-lattice problems.

FIPS 204

ML-DSA

Primary general-purpose digital signature algorithm. Derived from CRYSTALS-Dilithium.

FIPS 205

SLH-DSA

Stateless hash-based signature option. Larger and slower, but a different security foundation.

Selected

HQC

Future backup KEM based on code-based cryptography, selected by NIST for standardization.

Use-case decision map
Need shared secret?Use a KEM path. ML-KEM is the main standardized starting point.
Need signature?Use ML-DSA for general use, SLH-DSA where hash-based diversity matters.
Need transition safety?Use hybrid classical-plus-PQC modes where ecosystem support requires it.
Need backup diversity?Track HQC and FN-DSA status before production commitments.

Migration Roadmap

Good PQC migration is a staged engineering program, not a panic replacement. Start with inventory and crypto agility, then test protocol behavior, certificate sizes, device constraints, and operational rollback.

Migration sequence
1
DiscoverInventory algorithms, certs, keys, protocols, libraries, devices.
2
ClassifyData lifetime, exposure, criticality, regulatory pressure.
3
PrioritizeLong-life secrets, identity, signing roots, external endpoints.
4
Design agilityMake algorithms configurable; avoid hard-coded assumptions.
5
Test hybridMeasure latency, size, middleboxes, interoperability, rollback.
6
Roll outDeploy in waves, monitor failures, track standards changes.

Glossary

KEM

Key Encapsulation Mechanism. Lets two parties establish a shared secret over a public channel.

Hybrid mode

Combines classical and PQC mechanisms during transition so both must fail for the secret to fail.

Crypto agility

Ability to change algorithms, keys, parameters, and providers without redesigning the system.

CBOM

Cryptographic Bill of Materials: inventory of algorithms, certificates, libraries, keys, and protocol use.

Q-day

Informal name for the point when a quantum computer could break widely used public-key cryptography.

Harvest now, decrypt later

Attack pattern where encrypted traffic is collected now for possible future decryption.

PKI

Public Key Infrastructure. The certificate and trust-chain system behind websites, device identity, and signing.

HSM / KMS

Hardware and cloud services that protect keys and enforce cryptographic operations.

SIM / eSIM

Subscriber identity environments whose provisioning and lifecycle depend on secure keys, certificates, and channels.
