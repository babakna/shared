Why Quantum Computing Changes the Threat Model

A practical introduction to Shor, Grover, Q-day, harvest-now-decrypt-later, data lifetime, and why migration begins before a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists.

  • Explain why Shor's algorithm changes the public-key threat model.
  • Explain why symmetric encryption and hashes are affected differently.
  • Prioritize systems by data lifetime, exposure, and migration timeline.

1

What a cryptographically relevant quantum computer means

A cryptographically relevant quantum computer is not merely a small laboratory machine. It means a fault-tolerant quantum computer powerful enough to break widely deployed public-key cryptography in practical time.

The exact arrival date is uncertain. Migration planning does not depend on knowing the date exactly. Security planning often works under uncertainty: if the consequence is high and migration takes years, waiting for certainty is not responsible.

PQC migration is therefore a risk-management program. The question is not 'is Q-day tomorrow?' The question is 'which data and trust systems would still matter by the time migration is complete?'

TodaySystems still use RSA/ECC.
CaptureAttackers store ciphertext.
FutureQuantum capability may improve.
ImpactLong-life secrets may be exposed.

Concrete example

A government archive or telecom subscriber dataset may require confidentiality for decades. Even if Q-day is not near, migration may need to start now because the data remains valuable later.

Common pitfall

Do not treat uncertain timing as zero risk. Uncertainty affects planning, not the existence of the exposure.

Takeaway

Risk is driven by data lifetime plus migration time, not only by the forecast date.

2

Shor's algorithm versus Grover's algorithm

Shor's algorithm is the reason RSA, finite-field Diffie-Hellman, elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman, ECDSA, and EdDSA are considered quantum-vulnerable. These systems rely on mathematical problems that Shor's algorithm can solve efficiently on a sufficiently capable quantum computer.

Grover's algorithm gives a quadratic speedup for search problems. That affects symmetric-key and hash security margins differently. It does not create the same public-key collapse story. Larger symmetric keys and hash outputs can preserve strong security margins.

This is why a migration plan should not say 'all cryptography is broken.' It should say: classical public-key key establishment and signatures need replacement; symmetric and hash choices need adequate strength and review.

ShorPublic-key math at risk.
GroverSearch speedup.
Public keyReplace or hybridize.
Symmetric/hashUse strong margins.

Concrete example

RSA-2048 and ECDSA are public-key concerns. AES-256 and SHA-384/SHA-512 style margins are different discussions and are not replaced by ML-KEM or ML-DSA.

Common pitfall

Do not tell stakeholders that quantum computers break every encryption method equally. That creates confusion and bad priorities.

Takeaway

Shor drives public-key replacement; Grover drives margin review.

3

Harvest now, decrypt later

Harvest-now-decrypt-later is the attack pattern where encrypted traffic or stored encrypted objects are copied now and decrypted later when the attacker has stronger capability. The attacker does not need to break the system today to create future risk.

The attack matters most when data has a long confidentiality lifetime and is available to capture. Medical, legal, identity, telecom, financial, government, source-code, and sensitive business data are common examples.

This is the strongest practical reason to start planning before Q-day. A system that protects short-lived public information may not need the same priority as a system that protects secrets for twenty years.

Confidentiality lifetimeHow long secrecy matters.
ExposureCan attackers collect it?
Migration timelineHow long to fix.
PriorityLifetime > migration time = urgent.

Concrete example

Encrypted traffic carrying merger documents may be stored by an adversary today. If the key exchange is later broken and the documents are still sensitive, the damage occurs in the future but the capture happened now.

Common pitfall

Do not rank risk only by whether the system is internet-facing. Long-life internal data can be higher priority.

Takeaway

Long-life secrets create present-day urgency.

4

Practical risk triage

A simple first-pass triage scores systems by data lifetime, exposure, business impact, public-key dependence, and migration difficulty. It is not a final cryptographic review, but it helps decide where to start.

High-priority areas usually include external TLS, VPN, SSH, identity systems, code and firmware signing, certificate authorities, HSM/KMS dependencies, long-lived encrypted archives, and vendor products that will take a long time to update.

Low-priority does not mean ignore. It means inventory, monitor standards and vendors, and schedule migration with normal refresh cycles unless the risk changes.

HighLong-life + exposed + public-key.
MediumImportant but bounded.
LowShort-lived or low impact.
TrackInventory and revisit.

Concrete example

An internal dashboard with no sensitive data and short retention may be tracked. A firmware signing root used by field devices may need immediate architecture review.

Common pitfall

Do not make a single global deadline without understanding dependencies. Some systems need urgent work; others need planned refresh.

Takeaway

Prioritization prevents both panic and neglect.
