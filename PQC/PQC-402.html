What a CBOM is

A CBOM is a software bill of materials focused on cryptography.

Where an SBOM lists software components, a CBOM lists cryptographic assets and uses: algorithms, keys, certificates, protocols, libraries, hardware, and trust relationships. It captures enough to make risk decisions, and it is maintained continuously because apps, certificates, vendors, and libraries change.

SystemWhere crypto is used.
→
JobEncrypt, sign, exchange, hash.
→
DependencyLibrary, product, HSM, vendor.
→
RiskLifetime, exposure, readiness.

People often ask why they cannot just reuse their existing SBOM. The reason is that an SBOM answers "what software do we run?" while a CBOM answers "what cryptography does that software actually use, and how exposed is it?" A library can appear in your SBOM as a harmless dependency while quietly performing RSA key-wrapping on your most sensitive data. The CBOM is the layer that connects a component to the cryptographic job it performs and the data it protects — and that connection is exactly what you need to rank risk. Think of the SBOM as the parts list and the CBOM as the wiring diagram that shows which parts guard which valuables.

Everyday analogy

An SBOM is the inventory of every lock brand installed in a building. A CBOM tells you which of those locks are on the vault versus the broom closet, who holds each key, and which ones a known lock-pick can already defeat. Only the second view lets you decide what to re-key first.

Takeaway

A CBOM is the evidence base for every PQC decision. No inventory, no program.

2

Minimum fields that make the inventory useful

A row without an owner or a data lifetime cannot be prioritized or actioned.

Record ownership and enough precision to act. Algorithm names must be exact — "TLS" is not enough; record the key-exchange, the certificate signature, the protocol version, the library, and any pinned or legacy clients. Also record confidence: some data comes from automated scans, some from vendor statements, some from manual review. Confidence prevents false certainty.

Concrete example

Instead of "external API uses SSL," write: "external API, TLS 1.3, X25519 key exchange, ECDSA P-256 certificate, nginx/OpenSSL 3.x, browser + mobile clients (one pinned), 1-year data, owner Team Web, status: plan hybrid." That row can be prioritized and assigned.

Do not try to capture forty fields on the first pass — you will never finish. Start with the handful that actually drive a decision: system, crypto job, exact algorithm/parameters, dependency (library or product), data lifetime, exposure, owner, and PQC status. Those eight are enough to score risk and assign work. Everything else — certificate expiry, HSM slot, key custodian, ticket links — is valuable enrichment you can add once the skeleton exists and is being maintained.

Common pitfall

Hiding unknowns. An "algorithm: unknown" row is not a gap to paper over — it is a work item with an owner and a discovery method.

3

Discovery methods

No single method finds all your crypto. Combine several.

MethodFindsMisses
Network/TLS scanningPublic endpoints, certificate detailsOffline signing, internal-only, embedded
Source & dependency analysisLibraries, hard-coded parametersVendor appliances, runtime config
Config/certificate inventoryCert chains, key stores, cipher configUndocumented fallbacks
Vendor questionnairesProduct support statementsActual deployed configuration
Architecture interviewsHidden workflows, key custodyScale/coverage without tooling
Concrete example

A scanner finds all your public TLS endpoints, but only an architecture interview reveals the offline root key used to sign firmware — often your highest-risk, longest-lived asset.

The most dangerous crypto is the crypto you cannot see

The assets most likely to be missed are frequently the most critical: offline signing roots, an HSM's key-wrapping scheme, a hard-coded key in a legacy service, or a third-party library buried three dependencies deep. Because scanners cannot reach them, they stay off the inventory — and an asset that is not on the CBOM is an asset no one is migrating. Deliberately hunt the blind spots; do not assume "scan complete" means "coverage complete."

4

From CBOM to work queue

An inventory that does not produce actions is just documentation.

Each row should generate work: test hybrid TLS, enlarge a schema field, request a vendor roadmap, add certificate-chain monitoring, replace a weak algorithm, file an exception, or schedule device replacement. Each action carries an owner, target date, dependency, evidence requirement, and rollback plan. The dashboard then shows coverage, risk tiers, blockers, tests done, exceptions, and next-wave candidates — which is exactly the evidence PQC-401 asks for.

Takeaway

Inventory without action is not a migration program. The CBOM feeds the work queue; the work queue feeds the dashboard.

5

Scoring risk consistently

Gut-feel priorities collapse under scrutiny. A simple, repeatable score defends your ordering.

Every reviewer will argue their system is the most urgent. The way out is a transparent rubric applied to every row, so priority comes from the data, not the loudest owner. This course rates four factors 1–3 and sums them. It is a triage heuristic: record confidence, review edge cases, and never let the number override a documented safety, legal, or mission-critical concern.

FactorScore 1 (low)Score 2Score 3 (high)
Data lifetime< 1 year1–5 years> 5 years
ExposureInternal onlyPartner / limitedPublic internet
Public-key dependenceSymmetric/hash onlySome PK, easy to swapCore PK, hard to change
Migration difficultyYou control it, easySome dependenciesVendor/hardware-blocked

Tier mapping: 10–12 = Tier 1 (start/plan now), 7–9 = Tier 2 (next governed wave), and 4–6 = Tier 3 (monitor/refresh cycle). A high difficulty score raises urgency because it consumes runway; it does not mean the system is easy to finish.

Concrete example

An external VPN with 10-year sensitive traffic on vendor hardware scores 3+3+3+3 = 12 (Tier 1, urgent). A public marketing page scores 1+3+1+1 = 6 (exposed but nothing to harvest — low). Sorting the CBOM by this score produces your wave order automatically, and you can point to the rubric when someone disputes their ranking.

Common pitfall

Over-engineering the score. Four factors on a 1–3 scale is enough to sort a first pass. A 40-field weighted model that no one maintains is worse than a simple one people actually use.

6

Standards and keeping the CBOM alive

A machine-readable CBOM plugs into your existing supply-chain tooling — and stays current automatically.

The spreadsheet you built above is a great start, but a real program wants the CBOM in a standard, machine-readable format so it can be generated, diffed, and consumed by other tools. The industry direction is CycloneDX CBOM, an extension of the widely used SBOM format that adds cryptographic asset types (algorithms, keys, certificates, protocols) and their relationships. Expressing your inventory this way lets you generate it from build pipelines and scanners, track it in version control, and feed it straight into risk dashboards.

Automate the refresh

Wire discovery into CI/CD and periodic scans so the CBOM regenerates on every build and on a schedule. A CBOM that only updates when a human remembers is stale by definition — and staleness is exactly what a multi-year migration cannot afford.

Source-verification needed

CBOM tooling and the CycloneDX cryptographic schema are evolving quickly. Confirm the current field definitions and tool support against the CycloneDX project before standardizing your format organization-wide.

Takeaway

Start as a spreadsheet to learn the fields; graduate to a standard machine-readable CBOM (CycloneDX) generated automatically so the inventory never goes stale.

Hands-on labs

Use the builder above, then check your reasoning.

Lab 1 · Write a real row

Add a CBOM row (in the tool above) for an internet-facing payment API.

Which fields are the minimum to make it actionable?

Recommended answer System/endpoint, crypto job (key exchange + cert signature), exact algorithms and versions, library/product, client population (incl. pinned/legacy), data lifetime, exposure, owner, and PQC status/next action. Anything less cannot be prioritized or assigned.

Lab 2 · Find hidden crypto

Your automated scans report "coverage complete."

Where might crypto still be hiding?

Recommended answer Offline signing systems, embedded devices, HSM/KMS workflows, vendor appliances, custom key-wrapping, and backup/archive pipelines. Add architecture interviews to catch what scanners cannot see.

Lab 3 · Unknown to action

A row says "algorithm: unknown."

What is the correct next step?

Recommended answer Assign an owner and a discovery method with a due date. "Unknown" is a tracked work item, never an acceptable final state.

Lab 4 · Score two systems

System A: internal log store, symmetric encryption, 90-day retention, fully under your control. System B: public API with an ECDSA cert and 8-year customer contracts behind it, on a vendor gateway.

Apply the 1–3 rubric — which is higher priority?

Recommended answer System A ≈ 1(lifetime)+1(exposure)+1(PK)+1(difficulty) = 4 (low). System B ≈ 3+3+3+2 = 11 (Tier 1). System B wins clearly — long-lived, exposed, public-key-dependent, and partly vendor-gated. The rubric makes the ordering defensible instead of a debate.

Lab 5 · Keep it from going stale

Your CBOM was accurate at launch but is untouched six months later; certificates and libraries have since changed.

How do you stop the inventory from rotting?

Recommended answer Make it a machine-readable, regenerated artifact: adopt a standard format (CycloneDX CBOM), generate it from CI/CD and scheduled scans, store it in version control, and diff it over time. Assign the inventory an owner and treat the discover→record→score→act→monitor loop as continuous, not a one-time exercise.
