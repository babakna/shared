1. NIST-standardized starting point

The first production planning baseline is the NIST FIPS set: ML-KEM for key establishment, ML-DSA for general signatures, and SLH-DSA as a stateless hash-based signature option.

Concept flow
ML-KEMPrimary KEM from FIPS 203. Use for key establishment planning.
ML-DSAGeneral-purpose signature algorithm from FIPS 204.
SLH-DSAHash-based signature option from FIPS 205.
HQCSelected by NIST as a future backup KEM based on code-based cryptography.

2. Hybrid transition

Hybrid deployment combines classical and PQC mechanisms during migration. It is useful when compatibility, trust, or ecosystem maturity still depends on classical cryptography.

Relationship map
Why hybrid

Reduces transition risk while implementations mature.

Where used

TLS handshakes, VPNs, service meshes, and experiments.

What to test

Latency, handshake size, middleboxes, CPU, memory, logging, rollback.

What not to assume

Hybrid is not a free switch; protocol details matter.

3. Protocol impact

PQC can increase key sizes, signatures, certificates, and handshake payloads. Protocols with tight message-size limits or old parsers need special attention.

Decision matrix
TLS and HTTPS

Key exchange and certificates are primary impact points.

VPN and IPsec

Gateway compatibility, policy, and hardware acceleration matter.

SSH

Host keys, user keys, and key exchange algorithms need lifecycle planning.

Code signing

Long-term signature verification may outlive current algorithms.

4. Certificate and PKI issues

Certificates are not just files. They are used by browsers, servers, devices, cloud services, internal PKI, mobile networks, and software pipelines. Larger PQC signatures can change chain size and parser behavior.

CA readiness

Track CA support and issuance policies.

Client readiness

Old clients may reject new algorithms or large objects.

CT/logging

Certificate transparency and logging systems must parse new forms.

Revocation

OCSP/CRL and short-lived cert strategy may need updates.
