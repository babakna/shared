Protocols in Practice

Algorithms live inside protocols, and protocols live inside ecosystems. This module shows exactly where PQC appears in the systems you use daily — and why the surrounding parts, not the crypto, usually break first.

  • Locate the PQC change points in TLS, SSH, VPN, email, and code signing.
  • Predict what breaks when the ecosystem is ignored.
  • Read a real command that negotiates a hybrid PQC handshake.

Try it: where does PQC plug into each protocol?

Each protocol changes in a different place. Step through the map.

Pattern

In every protocol the crypto swap is the easy 20%. The other 80% is intermediaries, versions, sizes, and verifiers.

1

TLS and HTTPS

TLS is the highest-traffic migration and the best-tooled today.

Modern stacks support hybrid key exchange combining X25519 with ML-KEM. You can even negotiate it from the command line to see it work:

# Negotiate a hybrid X25519+ML-KEM-768 group with OpenSSL 3.5+ (or oqs-provider)
openssl s_client -connect example.com:443 \
  -groups X25519MLKEM768 -tls1_3

# Look for the negotiated group in the output:
#   Negotiated TLS1.3 group: X25519MLKEM768
Concrete example

Major browsers and CDNs already negotiate hybrid ML-KEM for a large share of traffic. Certificates, however, still mostly use classical signatures because PQC certificate chains are bigger and the whole PKI must catch up first (see PQC-102 and PQC-001).

Common pitfall

Enabling PQC on the server and declaring victory. Test through your real CDN, WAF, load balancer, and oldest supported clients — that is where larger handshakes fail.

2

SSH and VPN

Both are key-exchange-heavy and both have long-lived, slow-moving endpoints.

OpenSSH added post-quantum hybrid key exchange (e.g. an ML-KEM/X25519 method) and now defaults to it in recent versions — a great example of a quiet, successful migration. VPNs based on IKEv2 can carry PQC key exchange, sometimes using multiple key-exchange payloads, but appliance firmware often lags.

Concrete example

You upgrade servers to a PQC-capable SSH, but a fleet of old jump hosts silently falls back to classical key exchange. Without monitoring which group was actually negotiated, you would believe you migrated when you did not.

3

Email and messaging trust

Email is the hardest "easy" case: ancient, federated, and full of long-lived stored data.

S/MIME and PGP use signatures and key establishment that both move to PQC. But mail is stored for years, read by a huge variety of clients, and depends on directories/key servers to distribute keys. Modern secure messengers (Signal-style) have moved faster: some added PQC key establishment to their key-agreement ratchets, precisely because their clients update frequently and centrally.

Takeaway

The more centrally and frequently a system's clients update, the easier PQC is. Federated, long-retention systems (email) are the slowest.

4

Code signing and package ecosystems

Signatures again — and here the verifiers are everywhere and outlive the signer.

Firmware, OS packages, container images, app-store binaries, and software releases are all signed. Migration is gated by verifiers: bootloaders, package managers, app stores, and update clients must all accept the new signature algorithm before you can enforce it. Some ecosystems adopt PQC by adding a parallel signature alongside the classical one during transition.

Go deeper: dual-signing during transition

A common pattern: attach both a classical signature and a PQC signature to each artifact. New verifiers check the PQC one; old verifiers keep using the classical one. Once all verifiers support PQC, the classical signature is dropped. This trades bigger artifacts and more signing work for a safe, gradual cutover — and it only works if your artifact format allows multiple signatures.

Common pitfall

Enforcing PQC-only signing before the long tail of verifiers can validate it — instantly bricking updates for older devices.

Hands-on labs

Decide first, then reveal.

Lab 1 · "We turned on PQC in TLS"

A team enabled hybrid ML-KEM on their load balancer and reported the migration done.

What evidence would actually prove it, and what did they likely miss?

Recommended answer Prove it by logging the negotiated group across real clients and paths, not just a lab curl. They likely missed old clients/middleboxes that downgrade, and the certificate signatures (still classical). Key exchange PQC is necessary but not the whole story.

Lab 2 · VPN appliance

Your VPN vendor says PQC support is "coming next year."

What can you do now?

Recommended answer Inventory it, record the dependency and data lifetime, add it to procurement/roadmap tracking, and consider compensating controls (segmentation, shorter-lived data) until the firmware supports PQC key exchange.

Lab 3 · Firmware signing cutover

You want to require PQC signatures on firmware for a fleet with 8-year-old devices in the field.

How do you avoid bricking the old fleet?

Recommended answer Dual-sign (classical + PQC) during transition so old bootloaders keep validating, roll PQC-capable bootloaders through the fleet, then drop the classical signature only once verification coverage is confirmed.
