Protocols in Practice

Algorithms live inside protocols, and protocols live inside ecosystems. This module shows exactly where PQC appears in the systems you use daily — and why the surrounding parts, not the crypto, usually break first.

  • Locate the PQC change points in TLS, SSH, VPN, email, and code signing.
  • Predict what breaks when the ecosystem is ignored.
  • Read a real command that negotiates a hybrid PQC handshake.

Try it: where does PQC plug into each protocol?

Each protocol changes in a different place. Step through the map.

Pattern

In every protocol the crypto swap is the easy 20%. The other 80% is intermediaries, versions, sizes, and verifiers.

1

TLS and HTTPS

TLS is the highest-traffic migration and the best-tooled today.

Modern stacks support hybrid key exchange combining X25519 with ML-KEM. You can even negotiate it from the command line to see it work:

# Negotiate a hybrid X25519+ML-KEM-768 group with OpenSSL 3.5+ (or oqs-provider)
openssl s_client -connect example.com:443 \
  -groups X25519MLKEM768 -tls1_3

# Look for the negotiated group in the output:
#   Negotiated TLS1.3 group: X25519MLKEM768
Concrete example

Major browsers and CDNs already negotiate hybrid ML-KEM for a large share of traffic. Certificates, however, still mostly use classical signatures because PQC certificate chains are bigger and the whole PKI must catch up first (see PQC-102 and PQC-001).

Why is key exchange already common but PQC certificates lagging? Size. A hybrid key-share adds about a kilobyte to one message; a PQC certificate chain can add many kilobytes to every handshake, because a chain carries a leaf, one or more intermediates, and the signatures binding them — each of which grows. That inflates the handshake, stresses the initial-window and packet-loss behavior of TCP/QUIC, and can trip certificate-transparency logs and old parsers. So the ecosystem is doing the cheaper half (key exchange) first and staging the harder half (certificates) behind it.

Common pitfall

Enabling PQC on the server and declaring victory. Test through your real CDN, WAF, load balancer, and oldest supported clients — that is where larger handshakes fail.

2

SSH and VPN

Both are key-exchange-heavy and both have long-lived, slow-moving endpoints.

OpenSSH added post-quantum hybrid key exchange (e.g. an ML-KEM/X25519 method) and now defaults to it in recent versions — a great example of a quiet, successful migration. VPNs based on IKEv2 can carry PQC key exchange, sometimes using multiple key-exchange payloads, but appliance firmware often lags.

Concrete example

You upgrade servers to a PQC-capable SSH, but a fleet of old jump hosts silently falls back to classical key exchange. Without monitoring which group was actually negotiated, you would believe you migrated when you did not.

That silent-fallback trap is worth dwelling on, because it is the single most common way a PQC rollout looks finished while leaving you exposed. Most protocols are polite: if two endpoints cannot agree on the new algorithm, they quietly negotiate down to something both understand rather than failing. That politeness is great for uptime and terrible for security assurance, because "no errors" can mean "everyone upgraded" or "everyone fell back" — and you cannot tell the two apart without measuring. The fix is to log the negotiated group or algorithm on every connection and alert when the classical path is still being used. Until you measure it, your migration status is a hope, not a fact.

Rule of thumb

For any migration, decide in advance the one metric that proves success — usually "share of connections that negotiated the PQC algorithm." If you cannot see that number on a dashboard, you are not done, no matter what the config files say.

3

Email and messaging trust

Email is the hardest "easy" case: ancient, federated, and full of long-lived stored data.

S/MIME and PGP use signatures and key establishment that both move to PQC. But mail is stored for years, read by a huge variety of clients, and depends on directories/key servers to distribute keys. Modern secure messengers (Signal-style) have moved faster: some added PQC key establishment to their key-agreement ratchets, precisely because their clients update frequently and centrally.

Two live examples show where email is heading. Apple's iMessage moved to PQ3 and Signal introduced PQXDH, both adding post-quantum key establishment to their ongoing key-agreement (so even future messages in a long-lived conversation get fresh PQC protection). They could do this quickly precisely because their clients are centrally distributed and auto-update. Email's S/MIME and PGP will get there too, but only as clients, directories, and archive formats catch up — which is measured in years, not release cycles.

Concrete example

A signed email from 2026 may still need to be verified in 2040 for a legal dispute. If it was signed with a classical algorithm that is broken by then, its evidentiary value collapses. This is why archived, signed mail is a genuine harvest-and-forge concern, not just a key-exchange one.

There is a useful rule of thumb hiding in these examples. Ask two questions about any system: who controls the clients, and how long does the data live. A messenger controls its own app and its data is ephemeral, so it can flip to PQC in a single release. Email controls neither — anyone can run any client, and messages sit in archives for a decade — so it moves at the speed of the slowest widely-used client. Every protocol in this module sits somewhere on that grid, and where it sits predicts how painful its migration will be far better than any technical detail of the algorithm itself.

Everyday analogy

It is the difference between updating an app you built for your own staff versus changing the rules of the postal service. One is a Tuesday deployment; the other is a multi-year negotiation with everyone who has ever owned a mailbox. Same idea, wildly different difficulty — and that gap is governed by control and longevity, not cryptography.

Takeaway

The more centrally and frequently a system's clients update, the easier PQC is. Federated, long-retention systems (email) are the slowest.

4

Code signing and package ecosystems

Signatures again — and here the verifiers are everywhere and outlive the signer.

Firmware, OS packages, container images, app-store binaries, and software releases are all signed. Migration is gated by verifiers: bootloaders, package managers, app stores, and update clients must all accept the new signature algorithm before you can enforce it. Some ecosystems adopt PQC by adding a parallel signature alongside the classical one during transition.

This connects directly to software supply-chain security. The industry is converging on signed build provenance and software bills of materials (SBOMs) so you can prove what shipped and who built it. Every one of those signatures is a future PQC migration target — and, unlike a TLS session, a signed 2026 release may still need to be verifiable in 2040. The longer an artifact's trust must last, the stronger the case for a conservative choice like SLH-DSA and for dual-signing during the transition.

Go deeper: dual-signing during transition

A common pattern: attach both a classical signature and a PQC signature to each artifact. New verifiers check the PQC one; old verifiers keep using the classical one. Once all verifiers support PQC, the classical signature is dropped. This trades bigger artifacts and more signing work for a safe, gradual cutover — and it only works if your artifact format allows multiple signatures.

Common pitfall

Enforcing PQC-only signing before the long tail of verifiers can validate it — instantly bricking updates for older devices.

5

The universal migration pattern

Every protocol above follows the same four phases. Learn it once and you can migrate anything.

Whether it is TLS key exchange, SSH, a VPN tunnel, or firmware signing, safe migrations do not "flip a switch." They run classical and post-quantum together for a while, watch what actually happens on the wire, and only remove the old algorithm once the new one is proven everywhere. 6

Proving the rollout actually worked

A config that says PQC is on tells you nothing. Only the wire tells the truth.

The single most common failure mode in these migrations is the silent downgrade: you enable hybrid key exchange, but old clients or a middlebox quietly negotiate classical instead, and everyone believes the job is done. The fix is observability — measure what was actually negotiated, per connection, across real clients and paths.

# Check what a specific server actually negotiates
openssl s_client -connect example.com:443 -groups X25519MLKEM768 -tls1_3 \
  | grep "Negotiated TLS1.3 group"

# On an SSH server, confirm the kex method in use
ssh -vv user@host 2>&1 | grep "kex: algorithm"
ProtocolWhat to measureGreen signal
TLSNegotiated group per connection% of traffic on a hybrid ML-KEM group, trending up

Common pitfall

Measuring only success rate ("connections still work"). Connections working can mean classical fallback is silently rescuing you. Measure the algorithm, not just the outcome.

Hands-on labs

Decide first, then reveal.

Lab 1 · "We turned on PQC in TLS"

A team enabled hybrid ML-KEM on their load balancer and reported the migration done.

What evidence would actually prove it, and what did they likely miss?

Recommended answer Prove it by logging the negotiated group across real clients and paths, not just a lab curl. They likely missed old clients/middleboxes that downgrade, and the certificate signatures (still classical). Key exchange PQC is necessary but not the whole story.

Lab 2 · VPN appliance

Your VPN vendor says PQC support is "coming next year."

What can you do now?

Recommended answer Inventory it, record the dependency and data lifetime, add it to procurement/roadmap tracking, and consider compensating controls (segmentation, shorter-lived data) until the firmware supports PQC key exchange.

Lab 3 · Firmware signing cutover

You want to require PQC signatures on firmware for a fleet with 8-year-old devices in the field.

How do you avoid bricking the old fleet?

Recommended answer Dual-sign (classical + PQC) during transition so old bootloaders keep validating, roll PQC-capable bootloaders through the fleet, then drop the classical signature only once verification coverage is confirmed.

Lab 4 · Which phase are you in?

A team has enabled hybrid ML-KEM on all servers and dashboards show 40% of traffic on the hybrid group, climbing weekly.

Which migration phase is this, and what must happen before removing classical?

Recommended answer This is phase 3 (monitor) — "run both" is live and they are measuring the negotiated group. Before phase 4 (retire classical), the hybrid share must reach effectively 100% of supported clients, or you will cut off the long tail still negotiating classical. Removing classical at 40% would break most users.

Lab 5 · The connections-still-work trap

After enabling hybrid TLS, an engineer reports "100% success rate, migration complete."

Why is that metric misleading?

Recommended answer A 100% success rate can mean classical fallback is silently rescuing every connection that cannot do PQC. Success rate measures availability, not which algorithm ran. You must log the negotiated group per connection to know whether PQC is actually protecting traffic.
