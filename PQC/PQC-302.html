Use-Case Labs

Concepts stick when you apply them. Each lab is a realistic system. Read it, make the call, then compare with a worked recommendation. Use the same four questions every time.

  • Apply a repeatable decision framework to any system.
  • Choose key-exchange vs signature migrations appropriately.
  • Spot the ecosystem constraint that actually gates each rollout.

The four-question decision framework

Before any migration decision, walk these four questions. Every lab below is just this framework applied.

Why lean so hard on a fixed set of four questions instead of judging each system on its merits? Because "on its merits" is how migrations drift into endless debate and inconsistent decisions. A framework does three things a gut call cannot: it makes decisions comparable (every system was scored the same way, so priorities are defensible), it makes them teachable (a new engineer can run it without years of crypto experience), and it makes them auditable (you can show a regulator or your own board exactly how a conclusion was reached). The four questions are deliberately ordered, too — job first because it fixes the algorithm family, then lifetime and exposure because they set urgency, then verifiers because they set the real timeline, then rollout because that is where good plans die if untested.

How to use the labs below

Resist reading the recommended answer first. For each scenario, actually write your four answers — job, lifetime×exposure, verifiers, rollout — then reveal and compare. The muscle you are building is the framework, not the memorized answer to five specific scenarios. By the fifth lab you should be able to predict the recommendation before you open it.

Lab 1 · Public website and API platform

A customer-facing web app plus a public REST API, behind a CDN and WAF, serving browsers and a 4-year-old mobile app.

Traffic is short-lived per session, but sessions carry auth tokens and some personal data. The mobile app pins certificates.

Where do you start, and what is the hidden landmine?

Recommended answer Start with hybrid ML-KEM key exchange at the edge (CDN/load balancer) — that is the harvest-now-decrypt-later protection. The landmine is the pinned mobile app and any middlebox that rejects larger handshakes: test the real client matrix and log the negotiated group. Certificate signatures stay classical until the PKI is ready. Job: key establishment; constraint: oldest client + pinning.

Framework applied

1 Job: key establishment (TLS handshake). 2 Lifetime×exposure: per-session data is short-lived, but tokens/PII cross the public internet — medium-high. 3 Verifiers/endpoints: browsers (easy), the pinned 4-year-old app and middleboxes (the constraint). 4 Rollout: hybrid at the edge, canary, log the negotiated group; success = high hybrid share with no handshake regressions.

Lab 2 · Cloud service mesh and workload identity

Microservices authenticate to each other with short-lived mTLS certificates issued by an internal CA (SPIFFE-style), rotated hourly.

You fully control both ends and the issuing CA. Certificates live for an hour.

Is this easier or harder than the public web, and why?

Recommended answer Much easier — a rare "fast" case. You own every endpoint and the CA, clients update centrally, and hour-long certificates mean no long-lived signatures to worry about. You can move both key exchange and workload-identity signatures to PQC (or hybrid) quickly, with mesh metrics as your evidence. Internal, centrally-updated, short-lived is the ideal migration profile.

Framework applied

1 Job: key establishment + workload-identity signatures. 2 Lifetime×exposure: hour-long certs, internal network — low harvest value, so this is about agility more than urgency. 3 Verifiers/endpoints: all owned, all centrally updated — no long tail. 4 Rollout: flip the mesh to hybrid, watch mesh telemetry; you can even reach pure-PQC internally ahead of the public internet.

Lab 3 · Messaging and collaboration

An end-to-end encrypted chat product with frequently-updated mobile and desktop clients and a central directory.

Messages may be retained for years on devices; clients auto-update within weeks.

Which job matters most, and what makes this tractable despite long retention?

Recommended answer The key establishment in the key-agreement ratchet is the harvest-now-decrypt-later target — add PQC (hybrid) there so captured traffic stays safe. Long device retention matters, but fast, central client updates let you deploy the change to nearly everyone quickly. This is why modern messengers led PQC adoption.

Framework applied

1 Job: key establishment (in the ratchet). 2 Lifetime×exposure: messages retained for years and sent over the internet — high harvest value, so key exchange is the priority. 3 Verifiers/endpoints: many clients, but they auto-update within weeks. 4 Rollout: ship hybrid key establishment in a client release; adoption follows the update curve. This is exactly the Signal PQXDH / iMessage PQ3 story.

Lab 4 · Data at rest and backups

A data lake and 7-year backups encrypted with AES-256, where data keys are wrapped by a KMS using RSA.

Someone panics: "AES is quantum-broken, we must re-encrypt petabytes!"

What is actually at risk, and what is the efficient fix?

Recommended answer AES-256 is fine (Grover only). The risk is the RSA key-wrapping: an attacker who harvests the wrapped keys could unwrap them post-quantum and then decrypt everything. Fix is cheap: move key wrapping to a PQC/hybrid KEM in the KMS and rewrap the data keys — no need to re-encrypt the petabytes of bulk data. Job: key establishment (wrapping), not bulk encryption.

Framework applied

1 Job: two jobs, but only the key wrapping (RSA) is public-key — the bulk AES-256 is symmetric and stays. 2 Lifetime×exposure: 7-year backups — long lifetime makes the wrapped keys a prime harvest target. 3 Verifiers/endpoints: the KMS is the single control point. 4 Rollout: add a PQC/hybrid wrap in the KMS, rewrap data keys; petabytes of bulk data are never touched.

Lab 5 · Firmware and device lifecycle

Industrial sensors with a 10-year field life verify firmware with an ECDSA signature checked by an immutable bootloader.

The bootloader's verification key and algorithm are burned in at manufacture and cannot be updated in the field.

What is the hardest problem here, and what must change now?

Recommended answer The hardest problem is the immutable verifier: you cannot teach existing bootloaders a new algorithm, so devices already in the field may be unfixable. What must change now is new hardware/bootloaders designed for agility (support PQC signatures, or dual verification) plus a plan for the installed base (compensating controls, planned replacement). Signatures + long life + immutable verifier is the worst-case combination — act earliest here.

Framework applied

1 Job: signature verification (firmware authenticity). 2 Lifetime×exposure: 10-year field life — the verifier will outlive the algorithm. 3 Verifiers/endpoints: an immutable bootloader — the worst possible constraint, because it cannot be updated. 4 Rollout: you cannot roll this out to existing units; the action is new agility-capable hardware plus compensating controls and a replacement plan for the installed base.

Lab 6 · Long-term document & legal archive

A records system stores digitally signed contracts and filings that must remain verifiable for 30+ years.

Documents are not secret (many are public record) but their signatures must prove authenticity decades from now. Signing is low-volume and centrally controlled.

Is this a key-exchange problem or a signature problem, and which algorithm fits?

Recommended answer Purely a signature problem — confidentiality barely matters. Because the trust must last 30+ years and signing is low-volume/controlled, a conservative choice is attractive: SLH-DSA (stateless hash-based) for its minimal, well-understood security assumption, or carefully operated stateful LMS/XMSS. Consider re-signing important records with a PQC signature now (while the classical one is still trusted) so their authenticity survives the transition. Large signatures are an acceptable cost for a low-volume archive.

Framework applied

1 Job: long-lived signatures. 2 Lifetime×exposure: 30+ year trust, low secrecy — a forge-later, not harvest-later, risk. 3 Verifiers/endpoints: future verifiers you cannot predict — favor a conservative, widely supported algorithm. 4 Rollout: dual-sign / re-sign key records now; size is not a concern at low volume.

Lab 7 · Payments network with HSMs

A financial messaging network authenticates transactions with signatures generated inside FIPS-validated HSMs, integrated with many partner banks.

Transactions are short-lived, but the HSMs are FIPS-validated, partners upgrade on their own schedules, and every partner must verify your signatures.

What gates this migration, and what is the realistic sequence?

Recommended answer Two gates dominate: HSM support for a FIPS-validated PQC signature (a hardware/firmware and re-validation dependency you do not control), and the federation of partner verifiers who must all accept the new algorithm. Realistic sequence: track HSM PQC + FIPS validation roadmaps as critical path; agree an algorithm and dual-signing transition with the partner network; run hybrid/dual-sign while partners upgrade; only enforce PQC-only once verifier coverage across the federation is complete.

Framework applied

1 Job: signatures (transaction authenticity). 2 Lifetime×exposure: transactions are short-lived, so this is about trust continuity, not harvesting. 3 Verifiers/endpoints: a whole federation of partner banks plus FIPS-validated HSMs — the binding constraint. 4 Rollout: dual-sign across the federation, gated by the slowest HSM and the slowest partner.
