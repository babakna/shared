Quantum Computing in Plain English

People say "quantum computers will break encryption" without ever explaining what a quantum computer is. Let's fix that with analogies, not equations — just enough to understand the threat clearly and avoid the hype.

  • Describe a qubit and superposition without any math.
  • Explain why quantum helps with some problems and not others.
  • Separate real capability from science-fiction claims.

Try it: a bit is a coin lying flat, a qubit is a coin spinning

Step through the difference. This single idea explains most of the confusion about quantum computing.

Myth to drop right now

"A quantum computer just tries all answers at once and instantly picks the right one." Not true. It holds many possibilities, but measurement gives only one result. Speedups come only when a clever algorithm makes the right answer interfere constructively. Most problems get little or no speedup.

1

Classical bits vs qubits

Everything your phone does is built from bits that are strictly 0 or 1. A quantum computer adds a new building block.

A qubit can be 0, 1, or a controlled mixture of both. Physically it might be a superconducting circuit, a trapped ion, or a photon — the hardware varies, but the behavior is what matters to us.

The power grows with scale. Describing the state of n qubits requires 2n numbers (amplitudes). Ten qubits: 1,024 numbers. Fifty qubits: over a quadrillion. This is why quantum machines can, in principle, explore enormous spaces — but only if you can extract a useful answer at the end.

Analogy

A classical computer checking possibilities is like one person walking every path of a maze one at a time. A quantum computer is more like sending a wave through the whole maze at once — but you only get to see where the wave is loudest, so you must design the maze so the exit is the loudest spot.

Here is the catch that keeps quantum computers from being magic. That 2n of amplitudes lives inside the machine while it computes, but you can never read all of it out. Measurement gives you a single n-bit answer. So the exponential space is real, but it is only useful if the algorithm can concentrate the "loudness" onto the answer you want before you look. For most problems, no one knows how to do that — which is why quantum computers are not general-purpose speed demons.

Just how big is 2n?

50 qubits describe ~1.1 quadrillion amplitudes — already beyond a large supercomputer's memory. 300 qubits describe more amplitudes than there are atoms in the observable universe. Yet measuring those 300 qubits still yields just 300 bits. The gap between "how much structure is inside" and "how little you can read out" is the entire art of quantum algorithm design.

Myth to drop right now

The popular line that "a qubit is 0 and 1 at the same time, so a quantum computer just tries all answers in parallel and picks the right one" is wrong — and believing it will mislead you about the entire threat. If that were true, quantum computers would instantly solve almost everything, and they emphatically do not. The truth is subtler: the machine holds all those possibilities internally, but reading the result collapses it to one outcome. Speedups only appear for the rare problems where clever interference can make the desired answer overwhelmingly likely before you measure. Factoring (Shor) is one of those rare problems; most tasks are not.

This is exactly why post-quantum cryptography is a targeted replacement, not a panic. Quantum computers do not break "all encryption." They break the specific public-key schemes whose security rests on a hidden mathematical structure that interference can expose — and they only dent symmetric ciphers and hashes, which you fix by using larger sizes. Holding that distinction firmly, from your very first module, is what will keep you calm and precise while others around you overreact to headlines.

2

Superposition, entanglement, and interference

Three words do the heavy lifting. You already met superposition; here are the other two.

Superposition

A qubit is a blend of 0 and 1 until measured, with amplitudes controlling the odds of each outcome.

Entanglement

Qubits can be linked so their outcomes are correlated no matter the distance. This lets operations act on the whole system's structure at once.

Interference

Amplitudes can add up or cancel out, like ripples on water. Good algorithms cancel the wrong answers and reinforce the right one.

Interference is the single most important word here, and the least understood. It is what separates a quantum computer from "a computer that guesses randomly." A random guess spreads its chances evenly across all answers; a good quantum algorithm sculpts those chances so almost all of them cancel and one survives. The picture below shows the difference.

Cryptography is threatened not because quantum computers are "faster at everything," but because a few specific algorithms use interference to solve the exact math problems that certain cryptography depends on. That is the bridge to the next section.

3

What quantum computers are actually good at

Two algorithms matter for security. Everything else is mostly a research and modelling story.

AlgorithmWhat it doesEffect on cryptography
Shor's algorithmEfficiently finds the hidden period in a math structure — which cracks integer factoring and discrete logarithms.Breaks RSA, Diffie-Hellman, and elliptic-curve crypto. This is the core reason PQC exists.
Grover's algorithmSearches an unsorted space with a roughly square-root speedup (N → √N tries).Weakens symmetric keys and hashes. AES-256 keeps a strong margin; short keys should grow.
Concrete example

Shor turns "factoring a 2048-bit RSA number," which is effectively impossible for classical computers, into something a large enough fault-tolerant quantum computer could do in hours or days. Grover only halves the effective strength of a symmetric key, so AES-256 still behaves like ~128-bit security — still far out of reach.

Outside cryptography, quantum computers are genuinely promising for a narrow set of problems where nature itself is quantum: simulating molecules and materials (batteries, catalysts, drugs), certain optimization problems, and some machine-learning subroutines. These are exciting and real. But note what is not on the list: they will not make your spreadsheet, database, web server, or video game meaningfully faster. "Quantum" is a specialized accelerator, not a faster CPU.

Keep it in proportion

The security story is dominated by exactly one algorithm — Shor — against exactly one family — public-key. Everything else quantum computing might do is irrelevant to your migration. If you remember only that, you will not be fooled by breathless claims that "quantum breaks all encryption."

Takeaway

Shor drives replacement of public-key crypto. Grover drives bigger keys/hashes, not replacement. That asymmetry shapes the entire migration.

4

What quantum computers can NOT do

Cutting through hype is part of understanding the threat.

  • They are not just fast classical computers. For most everyday tasks they offer no advantage — and are far slower and more fragile.
  • They do not break AES-256 or SHA-2/3 outright. Grover only chips at the margin; doubling key length restores safety.
  • They cannot read a superposition's full contents. You get one measured answer, so brute-forcing "all passwords at once" is a myth.
  • They are extremely error-prone today. Qubits lose their state (decoherence) in microseconds and need heavy error correction.
  • They do not speed up ordinary software. Databases, web servers, spreadsheets, and games get no benefit — the speedups apply only to a narrow class of structured problems.
  • They cannot "download your data" from the future. The only reason today's data is at risk is that an attacker can store your encrypted traffic now and decrypt it later — the machine still has to do real work on captured ciphertext.
  • They will not run in your pocket. Leading machines need near-absolute-zero cooling or ultra-high vacuum and fill a room. The threat is a handful of large facilities, not a phone.
The decoherence problem, concretely

A superconducting qubit may hold its state for well under a millisecond before noise scrambles it. Breaking RSA-2048 requires billions of precise operations run reliably. Bridging that gap needs error correction so good that many noisy physical qubits act as one stable logical qubit — which is why the physical-qubit numbers must be so enormous.

Nuance

The honest summary: a cryptographically relevant quantum computer (CRQC) does not exist yet, and estimates of its arrival vary widely. But you migrate based on data lifetime + migration time, not on a confident date — that is PQC-101.

5

How far away is a threatening machine?

Today's chips have hundreds to low-thousands of physical qubits. Breaking RSA-2048 needs something very different.

The gap is error correction. Physical qubits are noisy, so many of them are combined to form one reliable logical qubit. Published estimates to break RSA-2048 involve on the order of thousands of logical qubits, which could mean millions of physical qubits — well beyond current hardware.

Why plan now anyway

Two reasons. First, "harvest now, decrypt later": data you send today can be stored and decrypted after a CRQC arrives. Second, replacing cryptography across a large organization takes years. If your data must stay secret longer than "time to a CRQC minus migration time," you are already behind. PQC-101 gives you a calculator for exactly this.

Takeaway

You do not need to predict the date. You need to know your data's lifetime and start early for the long-lived, exposed, public-key-dependent systems.

6

How Shor actually breaks RSA (no math required)

You do not need the equations — but the intuition makes the whole threat click.

RSA's security rests on one fact: it is easy to multiply two large prime numbers p and q to get a public number N, but fiendishly hard to go backwards and recover p and q from N alone. A classical computer trying to factor a 2048-bit N would take longer than the age of the universe. That "hard to reverse" gap is the lock.

Shor's insight is that factoring can be reframed as a different question: pick a random number and look at its powers modulo N — the sequence a, a², a³, … mod N. That sequence always eventually repeats, and the length of the repeat (its period) is secretly tied to the factors. Finding a period is exactly the kind of hidden, repeating structure that quantum interference is spectacularly good at uncovering. Once the period is known, ordinary school-level math turns it into p and q.

Why it hits ECC too

Elliptic-curve cryptography (ECDH, ECDSA) relies on a cousin problem — the "discrete logarithm" — which is also a hidden-period problem in disguise. So the same Shor machinery breaks it. That is why one quantum breakthrough threatens essentially the entire public-key family at once, rather than one algorithm at a time.

7

Reading a quantum-computing headline without being fooled

Quantum news is full of hype. Here is a translation guide you can use forever.

The headline says…What you should actually check
"New 1,000-qubit chip"Physical or logical qubits? Breaking RSA-2048 needs on the order of thousands of error-corrected logical qubits — which today would take millions of physical ones. Raw count means little.
"Quantum supremacy / advantage"Almost always a contrived benchmark chosen to favor quantum hardware — not factoring, not real cryptography. Impressive science, no crypto impact.
"Quantum computer broke encryption"Which encryption, and what size? Factoring a 5- or 6-bit toy number is a classroom demo, not a threat to RSA-2048. Look for the bit length.
"Error rates / gate fidelity improved"This is the metric that actually matters. Error correction, not qubit count, is the real bottleneck to a code-breaking machine.
"Experts say by 20XX"Is it peer-reviewed, and do standards bodies (NIST) share the view? Treat single vendor roadmaps as optimistic marketing, not a planning date.
Common pitfall

Reacting to qubit-count headlines with either panic or dismissal. Both are wrong. The right response is to migrate long-lived, exposed, public-key-dependent systems on your own schedule — regardless of this week's press release.

Takeaway

A "cryptographically relevant" quantum computer is defined by error-corrected logical qubits running Shor at scale, not by a big physical-qubit number in a headline. Judge the news by that bar.

Hands-on labs

Decide first, then reveal.

Lab 1 · Debunk the pitch

A vendor claims their quantum computer "instantly tries every possible key and returns the right one."

What is wrong with that claim?

Recommended answer Measurement returns only one outcome. Without an algorithm that makes the correct answer interfere constructively (which brute-force search does not), you just get a random guess. Grover gives only a square-root speedup, not instant answers.

Lab 2 · AES vs RSA

Two systems: one protects data with AES-256, another authenticates with RSA-2048.

Which is in more danger from a future quantum computer, and why?

Recommended answer RSA-2048 — Shor's algorithm breaks it. AES-256 only faces Grover's square-root speedup, leaving it effectively ~128-bit strength, which is still safe.

Lab 3 · Physical vs logical qubits

A headline reads "new chip hits 1,000 qubits."

Does that mean RSA is about to fall?

Recommended answer Almost certainly not. Those are noisy physical qubits. Breaking RSA-2048 needs thousands of logical (error-corrected) qubits, which may require millions of physical qubits. Qubit count alone is a misleading metric.

Lab 4 · Why ECC falls too

A colleague says "we use elliptic-curve crypto, not RSA, so Shor doesn't apply to us."

Are they right?

Recommended answer No. ECC's security rests on the discrete-logarithm problem, which is another hidden-period problem that Shor's algorithm solves just as efficiently. ECDH and ECDSA are broken by the same machine that breaks RSA — in fact often with fewer resources, since ECC keys are smaller.

Lab 5 · Interpret the metric

Two press releases: Chip A has 5,000 physical qubits with 1% gate error; Chip B has 500 physical qubits with 0.001% error.

Which is a bigger step toward breaking RSA?

Recommended answer Chip B's dramatically lower error rate is far more meaningful. Error correction (which needs low physical error rates) is the true bottleneck; a huge pile of noisy qubits cannot run Shor at scale. Fidelity beats raw count.
