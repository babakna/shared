Network, 5G/6G, SIM/eSIM, and IoT

Telecom is where every hard PQC problem meets at once: identity at massive scale, roaming across operators you do not control, tiny secure elements, and devices that live in the field for a decade or more.

  • Explain why telecom PQC is a cross-operator ecosystem problem.
  • Locate PQC touch points in 5G authentication and SIM/eSIM provisioning.
  • Plan for constrained, long-lived IoT and device fleets.

Try it: what happens when an eSIM profile is downloaded

eSIM provisioning is a chain of signed and encrypted steps across several parties. Step through where PQC will land.

Read the pattern

The identity/PKI and key-establishment steps are the PQC targets; the constrained secure element is the sizing challenge; and no single operator controls the whole chain.

1

Telecom PQC is an ecosystem problem

A phone call or data session crosses devices, SIMs, radios, cores, and often multiple operators — owned by different companies.

No single organization can "turn on PQC" for a mobile network. Device vendors, SIM/eUICC makers, network-equipment vendors, operators, roaming partners, and standards bodies (3GPP, GSMA) must move in a coordinated way. That is why telecom relies on standards and long lead times, and why crypto-agility is essential: you are negotiating change across a federation.

Takeaway

Treat telecom PQC like international PKI migration: the slowest interoperating partner sets the pace, so agility and standards alignment matter more than any single clever implementation.

2

5G and future 6G trust layers

5G already improved subscriber-identity privacy; PQC extends that trajectory into 6G-era design.

5G introduced concealment of the long-term subscriber identifier (SUPI) using a public-key scheme (SUCI) so the identifier is not sent in the clear — that public-key step is a natural PQC candidate. Core network functions authenticate each other over TLS-based interfaces (SBA), which inherit the TLS PQC story from PQC-202. The subscriber authentication itself (5G-AKA) is largely symmetric and less directly affected. 6G is being designed with quantum-safe assumptions from the start.

It helps to sort the telecom crypto surface into three buckets, because they age very differently. Symmetric-heavy pieces — the AKA authentication and the over-the-air ciphering — only need larger keys (Grover, not Shor), so they are the least urgent. Public-key pieces — SUCI concealment, the SBA TLS interfaces, and every certificate in the provisioning chain — are the Shor-vulnerable targets that need new algorithms. And long-lived secrets — anything an attacker could harvest now and exploit after Q-day — set the urgency. Mapping each interface into one of these buckets is the fastest way to prioritize a telecom estate.

Concrete example

The SUCI concealment that hides your permanent identity uses elliptic-curve public-key cryptography today. A quantum adversary who harvested those exchanges could later de-conceal identifiers — a privacy harvest-now-decrypt-later case, motivating PQC there.

Why 6G gets it easier

Because 6G is being specified now, PQC and crypto-agility can be baked into the standards rather than retrofitted. That is the whole lesson of this course applied at ecosystem scale: designing for agility up front is far cheaper than negotiating a change across a live federation later.

3

SIM and eSIM lifecycle

The (e)SIM is a tiny, hardened computer with real constraints — and a long, multi-party trust chain.

SIM/eSIM provisioning (GSMA RSP) relies on a certificate hierarchy and secure channels, as you saw in the walkthrough. The challenges: the eUICC secure element has limited memory and compute, so larger PQC keys and signatures are a genuine fit problem; and the trust hierarchy spans device makers, SIM vendors, and operators who must all support the new algorithms.

To see why the eSIM is such a hard case, picture the constraints stacked together. The chip is deliberately tiny and cheap, with kilobytes — not megabytes — of room, so a multi-kilobyte ML-DSA signature is a real budget problem, not a rounding error. It is hardened against physical attack, so you cannot casually "just update it" in the field. Its trust chain reaches back through SIM vendors and the GSMA root, none of which any single operator controls. And the profiles it holds may live in a device for the better part of a decade. Any one of those factors would slow a migration; the eSIM has all four at once, which is why it is often the pacing item for telecom PQC rather than the flashy radio or core.

Everyday analogy

Upgrading the eSIM trust chain is like changing the locks on every safe-deposit box in a banking network where the vault doors were made by different companies, the boxes are welded shut, and the master key hierarchy is shared across the whole industry. You cannot just email a patch — everyone who touches the chain has to agree and re-tool.

4

IoT, firmware, and long-lived devices

Field devices combine every worst-case factor: constrained hardware, long life, and hard-to-update verifiers.

FactorWhy it is hard for PQCMitigation
Constrained compute/memoryLarge keys/signatures may not fit or may be slowChoose parameter sets carefully; hardware acceleration
10-15 year field lifeMust stay trustworthy across Q-dayDesign for agility; plan replacement waves
Immutable bootloader/verifierCannot learn a new algorithm in the fieldNew hardware with PQC/dual verification; compensating controls
Intermittent connectivityHard to push updatesRobust, resumable, signed update channel
Takeaway

For device fleets, the decision made at manufacture dominates. Build agility and PQC-capable verification into new hardware now, because you cannot retrofit an immutable verifier later.

5

Strategies for constrained devices

When the chip cannot hold or run a full PQC scheme, you have more options than "give up."

The size and speed of ML-KEM/ML-DSA are fine on a phone but genuinely challenging on a kilobyte-scale secure element or an ultra-low-power sensor. A few patterns repeatedly solve this without abandoning security:

StrategyHow it helpsTrade-off
Pick the job-appropriate schemeKEM verify/encaps and ML-DSA verify are cheap even when signing is notMatch the burden to what the device actually does (usually verify)
Verify-only on deviceDevices only check signatures; heavy signing stays in the cloudRequires the device role to be verification, not signing
Stateful hash signatures (LMS/XMSS)Tiny public keys, ideal for firmware verificationSigner must manage state carefully (never reuse)
Terminate PQC at a gatewayA capable gateway does PQC to the internet; a lightweight link protects the last hopAdds a trusted gateway to the model
Hardware acceleration / next-gen SENew secure elements add PQC support and speedA manufacturing-cycle dependency, not a field fix
Concrete example

A smart meter cannot afford to generate ML-DSA signatures, but it only ever needs to verify firmware. ML-DSA verification is fast and its public key is manageable, so verify-only PQC fits comfortably — and for the firmware root, tiny-public-key LMS/XMSS is an even better match. The expensive signing all happens back in the vendor's HSM.

Common pitfall

Assuming "too constrained for PQC" based on the signing cost. Most devices verify far more than they sign; verification is usually affordable, and the burden can be shifted to where the compute lives.

6

Coordinating the federation

In a system no one owns end to end, the migration is a negotiation as much as an engineering task.

Because a mobile session spans five independently owned links, an operator's PQC plan is really a program of alignment: pushing requirements into standards (3GPP, GSMA), writing PQC and crypto-agility clauses into device, chip, and equipment contracts, tracking each roaming partner's roadmap, and staging changes so that no link is forced to fall back permanently. The technical work is real, but the pacing item is coordination.

Concrete example

An operator wanting PQC-protected eSIM provisioning cannot ship it alone: the GSMA trust hierarchy, the eUICC vendors, and the provisioning-server (SM-DP+) vendors must all support the new algorithms and larger certificates first. The operator's most effective lever is often standards participation and procurement clauses — not code.

The operator's real toolkit

Notice how little of this is code. An operator's most powerful PQC levers are influence in standards bodies, precise PQC and crypto-agility requirements in supplier contracts, and disciplined tracking of every partner's roadmap. Engineering readiness matters, but in a federation the migration is won or lost on coordination.

Takeaway

For federated systems, invest early in standards influence and contract language. Those, plus crypto-agility, let you move the instant your slowest partner is ready — and not a release later.

Hands-on labs

Decide first, then reveal.

Lab 1 · Roaming partner lag

Your network supports PQC, but a roaming partner does not.

What determines the effective security of a roaming session?

Recommended answer The weaker partner. Interworking falls back to what both support, so the slowest partner sets the pace. Track partner roadmaps and use standards/contracts to drive alignment; agility lets you upgrade the moment they do.

Lab 2 · eUICC won't fit

A chosen PQC signature is too large/slow for the current eUICC.

What are your options?

Recommended answer Select a smaller/faster parameter set or family suited to the constraint (verification speed and size matter most), work with the secure-element vendor on next-gen hardware, and stage the trust-chain upgrade so servers and chips move together.

Lab 3 · 10-year meter

You are specifying a utility meter that must run for 12 years.

What is the single most important PQC decision, and when?

Recommended answer At design/manufacture: make the firmware verifier crypto-agile and PQC-capable (or dual-verify). You cannot fix an immutable verifier in the field, so the manufacturing decision dominates the device's whole life.

Lab 4 · Verify-only sensor

A battery-powered sensor is "too weak for PQC," but it only checks firmware signatures — it never signs anything.

Is it really too weak, and what fits?

Recommended answer Probably not. Signing is the expensive operation; verification is comparatively cheap. ML-DSA verification is fast, and a tiny-public-key stateful hash scheme (LMS/XMSS) fits firmware verification very well. Keep the heavy signing in the vendor HSM and let the device only verify. "Too constrained" is usually a signing conclusion misapplied to a verify-only role.

Lab 5 · Operator lever

An operator wants PQC-protected eSIM provisioning but its code is already ready.

Why can't they just ship it, and what is their most effective action?

Recommended answer The GSMA trust hierarchy, eUICC vendors, and SM-DP+ provisioning servers must all support the new algorithms and larger certificates first — the operator owns only part of the chain. Their highest-leverage moves are standards participation (GSMA/3GPP) and PQC/agility clauses in procurement, which pull the rest of the federation forward.
