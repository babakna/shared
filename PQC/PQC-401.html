Enterprise Migration Playbook

PQC migration is a multi-year program, not a project. This module is the operating model: how to inventory, prioritize, manage vendors, govern exceptions, roll out in waves, and prove progress.

  • Stand up a crypto inventory and prioritization model.
  • Manage vendor and contractual dependencies.
  • Run governed rollout waves with monitoring and evidence.

Try it: how a wave-based rollout actually runs

You do not migrate everything at once. Step through a realistic wave sequence.

1

Inventory is the first deliverable

You cannot migrate what you cannot see. The crypto inventory (CBOM) is the foundation of everything else.

The inventory records where cryptography is used, for what job, with which algorithm and parameters, in which library or product, protecting what data for how long, owned by whom, and its PQC status. PQC-402 is a hands-on workshop for building one. Without it, prioritization is guesswork and progress is unmeasurable.

Takeaway

Treat the inventory as a living product with an owner, not a one-time spreadsheet. Vendors, certificates, and libraries change constantly.

2

Prioritization model

Rank by risk, not by whatever is easiest to touch.

TierProfileExamplesAction
Tier 1Long-lived + exposed + public-keyExternal TLS, VPN, KMS key-wrapping, identity, firmware rootsMigrate first (Waves 1–3)
Tier 2Important but bounded lifetime/exposureInternal apps, medium-retention dataMigrate on refresh cycle
Tier 3Short-lived or low impactPublic content, ephemeral cachesTrack and revisit
BlockedVendor/hardware cannot yet support PQCAppliances, embedded fleetsException + compensating controls
Common pitfall

Setting a single company-wide deadline. Tier 1 needs urgency; Tier 3 does not; blocked items need a different track entirely. One date for all creates panic and waste.

3

Vendors, procurement, and contracts

Much of your crypto is inside products you do not control. Procurement is a migration lever.

  • Ask for roadmaps: require vendors to state PQC support timelines for the exact products and versions you run.
  • Contract language: add crypto-agility and PQC-readiness clauses to new and renewing contracts.
  • Evidence: prefer validated implementations and interoperability test results over marketing claims.
  • Exit/compensating options: know what you do if a critical vendor is late.
Concrete example

A VPN appliance vendor promises PQC "next year." You log it as a Tier-1-but-blocked dependency, add a PQC clause to the renewal, segment the network as a compensating control, and set a decision date to evaluate alternatives if the roadmap slips.

4

Governance, exceptions, rollout, and monitoring

A program needs owners, an exception process, and evidence — or it stalls invisibly.

Define who owns crypto policy, who approves exceptions (and for how long), and what evidence closes a migration item. Each action gets an owner, target date, dependency, evidence requirement, and rollback plan. A dashboard shows coverage, risk tiers, blockers, completed tests, active exceptions, and next-wave candidates.

Takeaway

Evidence beats assertion. "We enabled hybrid TLS" is a claim; "98% of sessions negotiated a PQC group last week, here is the dashboard" is evidence. Exceptions must expire, not become permanent.

Hands-on labs

Decide first, then reveal.

Lab 1 · The single deadline

Leadership wants "all crypto PQC by end of next year, one deadline for everyone."

How do you respond constructively?

Recommended answer Reframe to risk tiers and waves: commit to aggressive dates for Tier 1 (harvest-now targets), refresh-cycle dates for Tier 2, tracking for Tier 3, and an exception track for vendor-blocked items. Offer a dashboard so leadership sees real progress instead of a false uniform date.

Lab 2 · The permanent exception

A team requests an indefinite exception for a legacy system "because it's hard."

What do you require before granting it?

Recommended answer Time-box it with an expiry and named owner, require compensating controls (segmentation, monitoring, reduced data lifetime), document the residual risk and who accepts it, and set a re-review date. Exceptions expire; they do not become silent permanent risk.

Lab 3 · Proving progress

The board asks "are we actually migrating, or just talking?"

What evidence do you show?

Recommended answer Inventory coverage %, Tier-1 systems migrated vs total, share of sessions negotiating PQC groups, dual-signing coverage, open blockers with owners/dates, and active exceptions with expiry. Metrics from systems, not status decks.
