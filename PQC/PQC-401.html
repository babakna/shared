Enterprise Migration Playbook

PQC migration is a multi-year program, not a project. This module is the operating model: how to inventory, prioritize, manage vendors, govern exceptions, roll out in waves, and prove progress.

  • Stand up a crypto inventory and prioritization model.
  • Manage vendor and contractual dependencies.
  • Run governed rollout waves with monitoring and evidence.

Try it: how a wave-based rollout actually runs

You do not migrate everything at once. Step through a realistic wave sequence.

1

Inventory is the first deliverable

You cannot migrate what you cannot see. The crypto inventory (CBOM) is the foundation of everything else.

The inventory records where cryptography is used, for what job, with which algorithm and parameters, in which library or product, protecting what data for how long, owned by whom, and its PQC status. PQC-402 is a hands-on workshop for building one. Without it, prioritization is guesswork and progress is unmeasurable.

The most common reason migration programs stall in year one is not technical — it is that leadership cannot answer "how big is this and where do we start?" because there is no inventory. An honest CBOM turns an unbounded fear ("quantum will break our security") into a bounded, ranked backlog ("we have 214 cryptographic dependencies; 31 are Tier 1; here are their owners and dates"). That reframing is what unlocks funding and calm decision-making. Spend the money and time here first; every later wave gets cheaper because of it.

What "living product" really means

A vendor ships a firmware update that swaps a library; a certificate authority rotates an intermediate; a team adopts a new service that quietly embeds classical crypto. Each of these silently changes your exposure. A CBOM that is refreshed on a schedule (and wired into procurement and CI/CD) catches these; a spreadsheet built once and forgotten is stale within a quarter and quietly lies to you.

Takeaway

Treat the inventory as a living product with an owner, not a one-time spreadsheet. Vendors, certificates, and libraries change constantly.

2

Prioritization model

Rank by risk, not by whatever is easiest to touch.

TierProfileExamplesAction
Tier 1Long-lived + exposed + public-keyExternal TLS, VPN, KMS key-wrapping, identity, firmware rootsMigrate first (Waves 1–3)
Tier 2Important but bounded lifetime/exposureInternal apps, medium-retention dataMigrate on refresh cycle
Tier 3Short-lived or low impactPublic content, ephemeral cachesTrack and revisit
BlockedVendor/hardware cannot yet support PQCAppliances, embedded fleetsException + compensating controls
Common pitfall

Setting a single company-wide deadline. Tier 1 needs urgency; Tier 3 does not; blocked items need a different track entirely. One date for all creates panic and waste.

3

Vendors, procurement, and contracts

Much of your crypto is inside products you do not control. Procurement is a migration lever.

  • Ask for roadmaps: require vendors to state PQC support timelines for the exact products and versions you run.
  • Contract language: add crypto-agility and PQC-readiness clauses to new and renewing contracts.
  • Evidence: prefer validated implementations and interoperability test results over marketing claims.
  • Exit/compensating options: know what you do if a critical vendor is late.
Concrete example

A VPN appliance vendor promises PQC "next year." You log it as a Tier-1-but-blocked dependency, add a PQC clause to the renewal, segment the network as a compensating control, and set a decision date to evaluate alternatives if the roadmap slips.

4

Governance, exceptions, rollout, and monitoring

A program needs owners, an exception process, and evidence — or it stalls invisibly.

Define who owns crypto policy, who approves exceptions (and for how long), and what evidence closes a migration item. Each action gets an owner, target date, dependency, evidence requirement, and rollback plan. A dashboard shows coverage, risk tiers, blockers, completed tests, active exceptions, and next-wave candidates.

Takeaway

Evidence beats assertion. "We enabled hybrid TLS" is a claim; "98% of sessions negotiated a PQC group last week, here is the dashboard" is evidence. Exceptions must expire, not become permanent.

5

Roadmap, budget, and operating model

A multi-year program needs a named owner, a funding line, and a clear division of labor — or it dissolves into everyone's side project.

The most common way PQC programs fail is not technical: it is organizational. No single owner, no dedicated budget, and crypto scattered across teams who each assume someone else is handling it. Fix that first. Give the program an accountable owner (often the CISO office), a standing budget that spans several fiscal years, and an explicit RACI so every wave has someone responsible.

RoleOwnsTypical home
Program leadRoadmap, budget, reporting to leadershipCISO / security office
Crypto architectAlgorithm/parameter policy, agility standardsSecurity architecture
Inventory ownerThe living CBOM and its accuracySecurity eng / asset mgmt
System ownersMigrating their own services per waveEach product / platform team
ProcurementVendor roadmaps and contract clausesSourcing / legal

Budget for the non-obvious costs. The algorithm swap is nearly free; the money goes to discovery tooling, HSM/appliance upgrades and re-validation, extra compute and bandwidth from larger handshakes, vendor upgrades, testing across a huge client matrix, and the staff time to run a multi-year program. Fund it as an ongoing capability, not a one-off project, because you will do this again for the next algorithm.

Reusable dividend

Everything you build here — the inventory, the agility abstraction, the wave playbook, the monitoring — is exactly what you will need for the next cryptographic transition. Frame the budget as buying permanent crypto-agility, not a single quantum fix.

6

Standards, mandates, and timelines

External deadlines increasingly drive the program — know which ones apply to you.

PQC migration is shifting from "good idea" to "required." Several bodies have published direction that may bind your organization or your customers. The specifics evolve, so treat the dates below as a map of where to look, and verify the current text against the primary source before you commit to any deadline.

SourceWhat it drivesNote
NIST FIPS 203/204/205The approved algorithms themselvesFinalized Aug 2024
NSA CNSA 2.0Timeline for national-security systems and their suppliers (e.g. software/firmware signing early, broad adoption later this decade)Verify current dates at source
US NSM-10 / OMBFederal agencies to inventory and plan migrationApplies to US federal + contractors
NIST IR 8547 (draft)Proposed deprecation of 112-bit-security classical PKC around 2030, disallow ~2035Draft — source-verification needed
Sector regulators / PCI / EUIndustry-specific crypto-agility and PQC expectationsVaries by jurisdiction/sector
Source-verification needed

The exact milestone dates in mandates change as drafts are finalized. Use this table to know which documents govern you, then confirm the live dates with the issuing body — do not quote a specific year to leadership without checking the current source.

Takeaway

Map your systems to the mandates that bind them. A regulatory deadline turns "we should" into "we must by [date]," which is often what finally funds the program.

Hands-on labs

Decide first, then reveal.

Lab 1 · The single deadline

Leadership wants "all crypto PQC by end of next year, one deadline for everyone."

How do you respond constructively?

Recommended answer Reframe to risk tiers and waves: commit to aggressive dates for Tier 1 (harvest-now targets), refresh-cycle dates for Tier 2, tracking for Tier 3, and an exception track for vendor-blocked items. Offer a dashboard so leadership sees real progress instead of a false uniform date.

Lab 2 · The permanent exception

A team requests an indefinite exception for a legacy system "because it's hard."

What do you require before granting it?

Recommended answer Time-box it with an expiry and named owner, require compensating controls (segmentation, monitoring, reduced data lifetime), document the residual risk and who accepts it, and set a re-review date. Exceptions expire; they do not become silent permanent risk.

Lab 3 · Proving progress

The board asks "are we actually migrating, or just talking?"

What evidence do you show?

Recommended answer Inventory coverage %, Tier-1 systems migrated vs total, share of sessions negotiating PQC groups, dual-signing coverage, open blockers with owners/dates, and active exceptions with expiry. Metrics from systems, not status decks.

Lab 4 · No owner, no budget

Two years in, PQC work is stalled: no dedicated owner, and each team treats it as a side project.

What is the root cause and the fix?

Recommended answer The root cause is organizational, not technical. Fix it by naming an accountable program owner (typically the CISO office), securing a multi-year budget line, and publishing a RACI so every wave has a responsible team. Frame the spend as buying permanent crypto-agility, reusable for the next transition — not a one-off quantum project.

Lab 5 · Which deadline binds us?

A US federal contractor asks whether the quantum migration is optional.

How do you determine the binding timeline?

Recommended answer Map the organization to the mandates that apply: NSM-10/OMB direction for federal agencies and contractors, CNSA 2.0 for national-security systems and their suppliers, plus any sector regulator. Then verify the current dates at the primary source (they shift as drafts finalize). For a federal contractor, migration is not optional — it is schedule-driven, and the inventory is usually the first required deliverable.
